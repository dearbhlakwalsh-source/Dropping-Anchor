Dropping Anchor — how it actually works

Important: A, C and E in the anchor all happen at the same time, not in sequence. You acknowledge the thoughts and feelings while connecting with your body while engaging your senses — all simultaneously, cycling through 3–4 times slowly. This is a 2–3 minute exercise when done properly.

Don't skip the A. Russ Harris is explicit about this — if you drop the Acknowledge step, the exercise becomes a distraction technique, which is not the point. The anchor works because you stay aware of the difficult experience while also being grounded. Both at once.

A — Acknowledge: silently say "I'm noticing…" — "I'm noticing anxiety", "I'm noticing my mind racing", "I'm noticing dread." Keep noticing throughout the whole exercise.
C — Connect with your body: while acknowledging — press feet into floor, straighten your back, press fingertips together, stretch slowly, breathe slowly. Stay aware of thoughts and feelings while doing this.
E — Engage with the world around you: while acknowledging and connecting — look around, name 5 things you see, notice 3 sounds, notice what you can smell or taste. Give your attention to where you actually are.
The goal is not to make the storm disappear. The goal is to be steady in it — anchored to the present moment while the difficult experience is still there.

The E in Dropping Anchor vs the E in ACE — they're different.

In the anchor, E means engage with what's around you right now — your senses, the room, the present moment. It's about grounding in the here and now.

In ACE, E means engage in valued action — choose something to move towards that matters to you. It's about direction and meaning.

A
Acknowledge — in ACE
Notice thoughts, feelings, and physical sensations — without judging, fixing, or pushing them away. Take the stance of a curious observer.
"I'm noticing anxiety. I'm noticing my heart is racing. I'm noticing my mind telling me something bad will happen."
Why it works: naming an experience creates a small space between you and it. That space is where choice lives.
C
Connect — in ACE
Come back into your body and the present moment. Breathe slowly. Feel your feet. Look around.
"I am in this room. I am physically safe right now. The anxiety is in my mind, not in reality."
Why it works: anxiety lives in a projected future or a replayed past. The present moment is almost always actually okay.
E
Engage — in ACE
Choose to re-engage with something meaningful. Not to avoid anxiety — to move towards what matters.
"What would I do right now if I were acting from my values rather than driven by the anxiety?"
This is ACT's committed action: behaviour guided by what matters, not by avoidance.

Your situations
How the toolkit fits each one
Physical symptoms — chest, breathing, heart rate
›

Physical anxiety symptoms feel alarming because your body is doing exactly what it's designed to do — preparing you for threat. The problem is it can't distinguish between a real threat and a perceived one.

The sensations are real. They are not dangerous. A racing heart is not a heart attack. Tight chest is not stopping you breathing. Your body will regulate itself.

In the anchor: pressing feet into the floor and straightening your posture gives your nervous system physical signals of safety through your body — not through thinking. Keep acknowledging the sensations while you do this, rather than fighting them.

In ACE's Connect: hand on chest and slow breathing continues that regulation. "These sensations are real. They are not dangerous."

Dread with no name — unnamed anxiety
›

Unnamed dread often feels worse than named fear because your brain treats the unknown as maximum threat. If it can't identify the danger, it stays on high alert indefinitely.

You don't need to identify it to work with it. In the anchor's Acknowledge step: "I'm noticing dread. I don't know why. That's okay." You don't need a reason. Just notice it's there.

Looking around the room in the anchor's Engage step gives your brain actual evidence that the environment is safe — even when the feeling says otherwise.

Before a social situation — anticipatory dread
›

Anticipatory anxiety is your brain running threat-simulations before the event — imagining everything that could go wrong. It feels like useful preparation. It isn't. It's your mind rehearsing worst-case scenarios that almost never happen.

The version of the event your mind is showing you is not what will happen. You have been in social situations before and survived every single one.

Anchor phrase: "I'm noticing my mind predicting this will go badly. I'm noticing anticipatory dread." — name it, then connect with your body and engage your senses.

In ACE's Engage: choose something that moves you towards the situation rather than away from it. Avoidance makes anticipatory anxiety larger every time.

Night-time — when the thoughts get loudest
›

At night your brain has no external input to compete with anxious thoughts. They fill the space and feel more urgent and more true at midnight than they will in the morning.

You cannot think your way out of this at night. The analytical brain gets worse after a certain hour, not better. Resolution can wait until morning.

The anchor is especially useful at night — the E step of looking around, noticing sounds, noticing what you can smell, gives your brain real external input to latch onto instead of its own spiralling content.

What helps you in ACE's Engage: getting up and doing something small breaks the lying-there loop. Putting something on to listen to gives your brain gentle input so it stops generating its own. "I don't have to solve this tonight."

When it arrives with no obvious trigger
›

Anxiety that arrives without a clear cause can be the most disorienting — because your mind immediately starts searching for what's wrong, which often amplifies the anxiety further.

You don't need to find the trigger to use the toolkit. Anchor first — get grounded in your body and the room you're in. The anchor works regardless of whether you understand why the anxiety is there.

Useful phrase: "Something has triggered my nervous system. I don't know what. I don't need to know what. I can respond skillfully without knowing why."
